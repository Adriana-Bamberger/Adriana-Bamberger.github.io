The Fallout From Friendship

Friendship is one of my important values. It has been installed in me from growing up in a small rural community, working in scouts with all kinds of people, you'll make all kinds of friends out of want or just dire circumstance from extreme bad weather.
Last year a friend makes a decision that challenged my values. it forced me to confront what was truly important to me in a friendship.
For several years, I had been part of a tight-knit group of friends. We went on and shared adventures, celebrated milestones, and were always there for each other even when we didn't all live close.
One member of the group, who we'll call T (25, F), had been a close friend for three years. When another friend, J (25, M), decided to move across the world to be with T, we were all very excited.
However, things changed shortly before J's arrival in Auckland from the UK.
We discover T has began a relationship with O, an 18-year-old who was flat mates with two of our friends.
The age difference (18 + 25 nearly 26) made many of us uncomfortable, but we didn't speak up incase it would damage the friendship.
We hoped that maybe it was a one night fling and things would soon go back to normal. J was due to land that next weekend, T had been talking about them for years.
Unfortunately, things didn't change.
T continued her relationship with O, and it became clear that she was prioritizing it over her friendships and J.
Clearly this made things awkward and painful for J when he landed in NZ.
T, another one of our friends, and J where flatting together, verbal abuse grew and culminated in a situation where T lashed out at the entire group online, cut off contact and moved out of the flat for a month.
In the aftermath of this fallout, my friends and I was left feeling hurt, confused, and betrayed.
It took a month for us to work out what had caused the lash out.
It was a painful month, I questioned my judgment and wondered if I had done the right thing by not speaking up sooner.
However, as time passed, I came to terms with my decision.
While losing a friend was painful, I realized that I couldn't stay silent about something that I felt so strongly about. My values, such as loyalty and honesty, were more important to me than maintaining a friendship with someone who no longer seemed to share those values.
Thinking back on those few months, it taught me about the importance of standing up for what you believe in,
Even when it's hard.
It also showed me the importance of surrounding myself with people who share my values, and still talking with people who don't so that you don't end up in a confirmation bias loop.
While I still miss the friend I once knew, I'm grateful for the lesson.

Strengths and Limitations

Figuring out my strengths felt a bit awkward, so I took online quizzes and chatted with friends and family.
Apparently, I'm a pretty focused and determined individual who thrives on challenges with a strong sense of justice.
I'm extroverted, being decisive and direct comes naturally to me.
In terms of learning, My focus helps me power through tough concepts, and challenges keep things interesting.
My directness allows me to set clear goals and ask for help without beating around the bush.
On the other hand, sometimes I come across as blunt or stubborn. I can get so focused that I miss the bigger picture.
Sarcasm also flies right over my head, which can make me seem insensitive.
I'm working on being more careful of how I come across, soften my approach and recognize different communication styles.
Sure, asking for clarification might ruin a joke sometimes, but for me, it's better than causing misunderstandings later on.

Dealing with team resistance

When I was working in an outdoors store that was made up of a team of;
Half young Uni students (under 20) who don't know or care much about our products,
And half older people working full time with more experience in the individual field(s) of the tools and items we're selling, who had passion and personal bias about the products.
Their was tension and distance in the age gap creating a slow and difficult to navigate work environment.
I was young, but was working full time and had years of experience in our gears fields.
At the time my sense of justice might have been a little misplaced, and it defiantly wasn't my place to do as much as I did,
But I felt the need to do something, and I had the means to - so I tried something.
I started small, being more talkative to my coworkers.
I went out of my way to talk to EVERYONE. Really leant into the "personality hire" stereotype.
Over a few weeks I;

  • Dressed more "fun" we had uniform t-shirts but other than that the only rule was "be modest" so out came puffy colorful and sometimes even sparkly skirts, light up shoes and fun jewelry.
  • Started using the work group chat for celebrating fun little things ie; "Yay! I didn't find any stolen item tags in the changing room today!" or "Woah! Learnt a new hiking boots laces trick!"
  • Adjusted the store playlist to be more up-to date radio safe pop.

This was making work more enjoyable, and I could see that the part timers attitude towards work changed the most.
Their was still the issue of people actually having some passion for the things we're trying to sell.
Involving them I started running education sessions in the first 15 min of work when it was the quietest.
We'd use a white board to go over a particular sport, product, or just a hard question someone had delt with.
I'd break it down, answer questions and put on a funny roleplay for the team.
I noticed the more I could get them to laugh about an item, the more they'd pay attention to what I was saying and hence the more of that item we would sell.
Looking back on it, If I needed to do this again I would talk to my managers about what I noticed and what I wanted to do first.
Hopefully I would have received more support and thus better results.
Either way I'm very happy that I did it, the store became a happier and more connected team - I also met one of my best friends during doing so.
